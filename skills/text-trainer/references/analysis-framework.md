# Analysis Framework — 8-Pass Voice Extraction

Run these 8 passes sequentially against the collected writing samples. Each pass focuses on a different dimension of voice. Do not skip or combine passes — patterns that seem obvious in one pass often reveal nuance in another.

Take notes during each pass. The notes feed directly into the profile template.

---

## Pass 1: Global Read

**Goal:** Get an intuitive feel for the voice before analyzing it.

Read all samples straight through without stopping to analyze. After reading, write down:

- Your gut reaction: what kind of person does this sound like?
- 3-5 adjectives that describe the overall voice
- 3-5 adjectives that this voice is definitely NOT
- What's the energy level? (calm, intense, measured, explosive, steady)
- If this person walked into a room, how would they talk?

**Output:** A 1-2 sentence persona description and adjective/anti-adjective lists.

---

## Pass 2: Structural Scan

**Goal:** How do they organize their writing?

For each sample, note:

- **Length:** How long are their messages? Do they write long or keep it tight?
- **Paragraph structure:** Short stacked lines? Long flowing paragraphs? Single sentences as paragraphs?
- **Opening patterns:** How do they start? Jump straight in? Set context first? Ask a question? Make a claim?
- **Closing patterns:** How do they end? Call to action? Question? Statement? Just stop?
- **Transitions:** How do they move between ideas? Connectors? Line breaks? No transition at all?
- **Lists vs prose:** Do they use bullets/numbers or write everything in sentences?
- **Format preferences:** Any consistent formatting choices across samples?

**Output:** Structural pattern summary with specific examples from the samples.

---

## Pass 3: Vocabulary Analysis

**Goal:** What words define this person's voice?

Scan all samples looking for:

- **Signature words:** Words they use noticeably often (frequency matters — note if a word appears in 3+ samples)
- **Industry/domain words:** Jargon or terminology they're comfortable with
- **Casual vs formal vocabulary:** Where do they fall on the spectrum?
- **Words they NEVER use:** Equally important — look at what's absent. If they write about business but never say "leverage," "synergy," "optimize," that's a pattern.
- **Made-up or modified words:** Do they coin phrases, use slang, abbreviate?
- **Verbal tics:** Filler words or phrases they lean on ("look," "honestly," "the thing is," "here's the deal")

**Output:** Lists of signature words, absent words, and verbal tics with frequency notes.

---

## Pass 4: Punctuation and Formatting

**Goal:** How do they use punctuation and formatting as voice tools?

Look for patterns in:

- **Em dashes (—):** Do they use them? How often? What for? (interjections, asides, emphasis, list-like structures)
- **Ellipsis (...):** Present? What do they signal? (trailing thoughts, pauses, suspense)
- **Exclamation marks (!):** Frequency? Context? (excitement, emphasis, never)
- **Question marks:** How often do they ask questions in their writing?
- **Commas:** Heavy comma use (long, flowing sentences) or light (short, punchy)?
- **Periods:** Do they use sentence fragments followed by periods for emphasis?
- **Capitalization:** ALL CAPS for emphasis? Title Case? All lowercase?
- **Line breaks:** Strategic line breaks for emphasis? Double line breaks between thoughts?
- **Parentheses:** Do they use them for asides?
- **Quotation marks:** For quoting, for irony/emphasis, or not at all?

**Output:** Punctuation profile with usage patterns and examples.

---

## Pass 5: Sentence Pattern Analysis

**Goal:** What's the rhythm of their writing?

Analyze sentence-level patterns:

- **Average sentence length:** Count words in 10+ sentences across samples. Note the range.
- **Sentence length variation:** Do they mix short and long, or stay consistent?
- **Fragment usage:** Do they use sentence fragments? How often? For what effect?
- **Sentence starters:** How do they begin sentences? ("I," "The," "So," "Look," verbs, nouns, questions)
- **Rhythm patterns:** Do they build momentum (short → medium → long)? Alternate? Keep steady?
- **Parallel structure:** Do they repeat grammatical structures for effect?
- **Run-on tendencies:** Do they connect ideas with "and" and "but" or break them into separate sentences?

**Output:** Sentence pattern summary with representative examples and length statistics.

---

## Pass 6: Tone Mapping by Context

**Goal:** How does their voice shift across different writing types?

Group samples by type (tweets, emails, memos, Slack messages, etc.) and compare:

- **What stays the same:** Core voice elements that appear regardless of context
- **What shifts:** Formality, length, vocabulary, energy
- **Context-specific patterns:** Things they only do in tweets but never in emails, etc.

For each writing type represented in the samples, note:

- Typical length range
- Tone adjustments (more formal? more casual? more direct?)
- Structural differences
- Vocabulary shifts
- Any context-specific signature moves

**Output:** Tone map showing consistent core + context-specific variations. Only map types you have samples for.

---

## Pass 7: Anti-Pattern Identification

**Goal:** What does this person NEVER do?

This pass is about absence. Anti-patterns are as important as patterns — they define the boundaries of the voice.

Look for:

- **Phrases they avoid:** Common phrases or constructions notably absent from all samples
- **Tones they never hit:** Are they never sarcastic? Never self-deprecating? Never formal?
- **Structures they don't use:** Never use bullet points? Never write one-word sentences? Never ask questions?
- **Words conspicuously absent:** Industry buzzwords everyone else uses but they don't
- **Things they never do in openings/closings:** Never say "hope you're well"? Never use a greeting?
- **Formatting they avoid:** Never use bold? Never use emoji? Never write walls of text?

**Output:** Explicit list of things this person NEVER does in their writing, with evidence (or pointed absence of evidence).

---

## Pass 8: Signature Move Identification

**Goal:** What makes this person's writing instantly recognizable?

This is the synthesis pass. Looking across all previous passes, identify:

- **2-3 signature moves:** The things that, if you saw them in an anonymous piece of writing, would make you say "that's [name]." These are the intersection of frequency + uniqueness.
  - Could be a structural habit (always ending with a question)
  - Could be a vocabulary choice (a word they own)
  - Could be a punctuation pattern (em dashes everywhere)
  - Could be a tonal quality (the way they mix blunt and warm)

- **The "golden rule":** If you had to give someone ONE instruction to write like this person, what would it be?

- **Voice samples:** Select 3-5 of the best samples that most purely represent the voice. These become the "if in doubt, re-read these" reference for any skill consuming the profile. Annotate each sample briefly — what makes it a good example of the voice.

**Output:** Signature moves list, golden rule, and annotated voice samples.

---

## Assembly

After all 8 passes are complete, you have everything needed to fill in the profile template. Move to `profile-template.md` and assemble the final profile.

Key assembly principles:
- **Be specific, not generic.** "Uses short sentences" is weak. "Averages 8-12 words per sentence, frequently drops to 3-5 word fragments for emphasis" is useful.
- **Use their actual words.** Quote directly from samples whenever possible.
- **Quantify when you can.** "Uses em dashes" is less useful than "Uses em dashes in 80% of samples, primarily for interjections and asides."
- **Prioritize what's distinctive.** Everyone "writes clearly." What makes THIS person's writing different from anyone else's?
- **Anti-patterns are load-bearing.** A profile that says "never uses exclamation marks" prevents more bad output than a profile that says "writes confidently."
