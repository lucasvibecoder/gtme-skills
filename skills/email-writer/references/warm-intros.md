# Warm Intros & Inbound

Use this reference when there's an existing connection — referral, mutual contact, inbound lead, or re-engagement after a previous relationship. The relationship does most of the heavy lifting. Your job is to not screw it up.

---

## Core Principle

Warm emails need less persuasion and more clarity. The prospect already has a reason to pay attention (the referral, the inbound action, the shared connection). Don't oversell. Be clear about why you're reaching out and make the next step easy.

---

## Referral Follow-up

Someone introduced you or told you to reach out.

### If the Referrer Made a Warm Intro (Email/DM)

The introduction already happened. Don't re-introduce yourself from scratch.

```
{{first_name}} – thanks for connecting. [Referrer name] mentioned you're [specific thing referrer said — e.g., "exploring ways to scale outbound without adding headcount"].

[1 sentence on what you do that's relevant — keep it tight]

Would [specific next step] be useful? Happy to [low-commitment offer].
```

**Rules:**
- Reference the referrer by name — that's your credibility
- Use the referrer's framing, not yours — if they said "they need help with pipeline," say "pipeline," not "revenue optimization"
- Don't dump your full pitch — the referrer already pre-sold you
- Make the next step specific and low-friction

### If You're Reaching Out Cold But Have a Mutual Connection

No introduction was made, but you share a contact.

```
{{first_name}} – [mutual connection] and I [context on relationship — worked together at X, connected through Y].

[Your reason for reaching out — specific, not generic]

If [topic] is relevant, I'd love to [specific ask]. If not, no worries at all.
```

**Rules:**
- Be honest about the connection. Don't imply an introduction if there wasn't one.
- The mutual connection gives you permission to reach out, not permission to pitch.

### If the Referrer Said "Tell Them I Sent You" (No Formal Intro)

```
{{first_name}} – [referrer name] suggested I reach out. They mentioned [specific context].

[1-2 sentences connecting their situation to what you do]

Would it make sense to [next step]?
```

## Inbound Response

The prospect came to you — filled out a form, downloaded content, requested a demo, replied to outreach.

### Key Principle

They made the first move. Match their energy level. If they filled out a form with 3 words, don't reply with 5 paragraphs.

### Inbound Demo Request

```
{{first_name}} – got your request. Looking forward to showing you [product/service].

[If you have context on their company: 1 sentence showing you did quick research]

Does [specific time] this [day] work? I'll keep it to [X minutes] and focus on [most relevant aspect based on their form data or company].
```

**Rules:**
- Respond fast. Speed matters more than perfection for inbound.
- Propose a specific time. Don't say "when works for you?" — take the lead.
- If their form data gives you context, use it. If not, keep it short and get on the call.

### Inbound Content Download / Resource Request

They downloaded something but didn't request a call.

```
{{first_name}} – saw you grabbed [resource name]. [1 sentence on what's most useful in it].

If [topic of the resource] is something you're actively working on, happy to [low-commitment offer — share additional data, walk through a specific section, connect you with someone who implemented this].

Either way, hope it's helpful.
```

**Rules:**
- Don't immediately pitch a call. They downloaded content, not requested a demo.
- Add value on top of the resource. Don't just say "did you read it?"
- The CTA should be softer than a demo request CTA.

### Inbound Email / Reply to Outreach

They replied to your cold email or reached out directly.

**If their reply is positive ("interested," "tell me more," "let's talk"):**
- Respond quickly
- Propose a specific time
- Don't overexplain — they already expressed interest, get them on a call

**If their reply is a question ("how much does this cost?" "what's your process?"):**
- Answer the question directly. Don't dodge it to force a call.
- Then offer the call: "Happy to go deeper on this — would [time] work for a quick walkthrough?"

**If their reply is skeptical ("we already have something" / "not sure this applies"):**
- Acknowledge their position
- Ask one clarifying question that demonstrates you're listening, not pitching
- "That makes sense. Out of curiosity, how is [current solution] handling [specific challenge]?"

## Re-engagement After Silence

You had a relationship — maybe a conversation months ago, maybe a deal that died — and now you want to reopen it.

### Previous Conversation, Long Gap

```
{{first_name}} – we talked back in [timeframe] about [specific topic]. I know timing wasn't right then.

[Something that's changed — on your end, their end, or in the market — that makes this relevant again]

If [original topic] is back on the table, I'd love to reconnect. If not, no pressure at all.
```

### Previous Customer / Churned Account

```
{{first_name}} – it's been a while since we worked together. [1 sentence acknowledging the previous relationship without being awkward about why it ended].

[Something new you can offer or something that's changed]

Open to a quick conversation to see if there's a fit again?
```

### Met Once, Never Followed Up (Your Fault)

Be honest. Don't pretend this is normal outreach timing.

```
{{first_name}} – I should have followed up sooner after [how you met]. That's on me.

[Why you're reaching out now — something specific, not "I was just thinking about you"]

Still interested in connecting if you are.
```

## LinkedIn Connection → Email Bridge

When you've been chatting on LinkedIn and want to move to email.

```
{{first_name}} – moving this to email so it's easier to [share details / send resources / coordinate calendars].

[Continue naturally from where the LinkedIn conversation left off — don't restart the pitch]

[Next step or resource you're sharing]
```

**Rules:**
- Don't re-pitch. They already know why you're talking.
- Give a reason for the channel switch (easier to share files, easier to loop in colleagues, etc.)
- Keep it seamless — this should feel like the same conversation in a different place.
