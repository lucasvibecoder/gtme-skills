# Cold Outreach Frameworks

Use this reference when writing first-touch emails to prospects who don't know you. For emails to engaged prospects or active deals, see `deal-followup.md`.

---

## Before You Write

### 1. Assess Prospect Awareness (Schwartz)

Where does this prospect sit? This determines your hook.

| Awareness Level | Their Mindset | Your Hook Strategy |
|-----------------|---------------|--------------------|
| **Most Aware** | Knows your product, wants it, hasn't bought | Lead with offer/price — make it easy to say yes |
| **Product-Aware** | Knows your product, not convinced it's best | Lead with superiority — why you vs. alternatives |
| **Desire-Aware** | Wants the result, doesn't know your product | Lead with the result they want — name the outcome |
| **Problem-Aware** | Recognizes the pain, doesn't see connection to a solution | Lead with dramatized pain — make the problem vivid |
| **Unaware** | Not thinking about this at all | Lead with identity/tribe — call out who they are |

**Most B2B cold outreach targets Problem-Aware or Desire-Aware prospects.** If you're reaching out to Unaware prospects, you need a much softer, curiosity-driven approach — you can't pitch someone who doesn't know they have a problem.

### 2. Assess Market Sophistication

How saturated is this person's inbox with messages like yours?

| Stage | Market State | Your Strategy |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **Fresh** | You're first or early | Be simple and direct — state the claim plainly |
| **Competitive** | Others make similar claims | Enlarge the claim — bigger numbers, bolder proof |
| **Saturated** | Claims are unbelievable | Pivot to HOW — introduce your mechanism, the new way |
| **Very saturated** | Even mechanisms are known | Elaborate on mechanism — faster, surer, easier than others |

**Most B2B inboxes are Stage 3-4.** If you're selling outbound services to a VP Sales, they've seen your email 50 times this month. You need mechanism differentiation.

### 3. Select Campaign Angle

Based on what data you have and what feels true about this prospect:

| Angle | When to Use | Key Element |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Problem Sniffing** | You can detect a specific problem via data or research | Show the problem before offering the solution |
| **Poke the Bear** | You can challenge an assumption with a sharp question | Open-ended question that creates cognitive dissonance |
| **Stars Aligning** | Multiple signals must be true (large TAM, specific criteria) | Reach out only when all conditions are met |
| **Relevant Case Study** | You have strong proof for a very similar company | Niche the case study hard — same industry, same size, same problem |
| **Being Genuinely Useful** | You can provide value without asking for anything | Give something first — free audit, data, insight |
| **Two Sentence Straight to Point** | Offer is simple + you have a clear data hook | Cut to it. No fluff. |
| **Flash Roll** | You have a proprietary mechanism competitors can't claim | Show HOW it works in specific, technical detail |
| **AI Envision** | Usage varies by customer and you can show their specific use case | Use AI to paint their specific future state |
| **Great Lead Magnet** | You can offer something the market knows is valuable | Free version of a paid thing — not a webinar |

## Email Structure

### Single Cold Email

```
Subject: [2-3 words, lowercase, colleague-style]

{{first_name}} – [Why you, why now. Shows research. Appears in preview.]

[Poke the bear question OR state the problem/opportunity in 1-2 sentences.]

[Social proof — quantified, specific, relevant.]

[CTA — answer: "How do we make this worth their time?"]
```

**Rules:**
- Under 100 words total
- 5th grade reading level
- About them, not you
- First sentence answers "why am I getting this email right now?"

### Poke the Bear Questions

The most effective cold email hook. Creates cognitive dissonance — the prospect can't ignore the question because they don't have a good answer.

**Question stems:**

| Stem | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| "How do you know..." | Challenges certainty |
| "What ensures..." | Questions process reliability |
| "What happens when..." | Highlights failure points |
| "How do you account for..." | Exposes blind spots |
| "What's your process for..." | Implies they might not have one |

**Rules for good poke-the-bear questions:**
- Open-ended — never yes/no
- Non-accusatory — "How are you approaching..." not "Why aren't you..."
- Specific to their role and industry — generic = no impact
- Challenges assumptions without insulting intelligence
- You must know the answer (or have a strong hypothesis) before asking

**Psychology behind it:**
- **Cognitive dissonance:** The question clashes with their belief that they have this handled
- **Loss aversion:** Frame around potential losses, not gains ("How do you know you're not losing..." vs. "I can help you gain...")
- **Status quo bias:** Make the discomfort of doing nothing greater than the discomfort of change

### Subject Lines

**Cold outreach subjects:**
- 2-3 words max
- All lowercase
- Could have been sent by a colleague or customer
- Don't give away the message
- Spark curiosity to open, nothing more

**Examples that work:**
- "quick question"
- "saw your post"
- "[company] + [your company]"
- "pizza orders" (for a restaurant ordering company — 88%+ open rate)
- "open to this?"
- "via [referral name]"
- "[mutual connection]"

### CTAs

**The test:** "How do we make this worth their time?"

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Would you want to hop on a call?" | "If we could reduce OSHA fines with 2 hours of implementation, would you take me up on that?" |
| "Let me know if you're interested" | "Worth 15 minutes to see how this works for [their specific situation]?" |
| "Can I send you some info?" | "Would you watch a 3-minute video showing how we did this for [similar company]?" |

**Lower friction CTAs for later in a sequence:**
- "If I made a short video walking through this, would you watch it?"
- "Would it be helpful if I sent over the case study?"
- "Open to seeing how we did this for [company]?"

## Personalization

**Variables — use {{variable_name}} format:**

Always include: `{{first_name}}`, `{{company_name}}`

Context-specific (use what's available):
- `{{job_title}}` — their role
- `{{competitor}}` — a named competitor
- `{{metric}}` — a specific number relevant to them
- `{{technology}}` — tool/tech they use
- `{{signal}}` — the data point that triggered outreach
- `{{ai_generated_line}}` — AI-personalized line based on their profile/content

**Personalization quality matters more than personalization quantity.** One deeply researched, specific observation beats three surface-level facts.

## Full Examples

### Problem Sniffing + Poke the Bear (Hybrid)

**Subject:** quote from last year

{{first_name}} – saw Tavoron closed Seltrol and B.W. Clark last month. That's 3 acquisitions in 12 months.

Quick question: when one of your AMs preps for a call with a customer who's bought compressors from JHFOSTER and sensors from Sensors Inc — how long does it take to pull the full picture?

We build AI that consolidates ERP, CRM, and service history into instant call briefs. One industrial distributor cut prep time by 5+ hours per rep per week.

If your AMs are losing time to cross-system searching, worth a 15-min look?

### Stars Aligning

**Subject:** open roles

{{first_name}} – noticed {{company_name}} is hiring 3 new AEs while also rolling out {{technology}}. That combination usually means pipeline targets just went up but the data infrastructure hasn't caught up yet.

We helped a similar-stage company generate 40% more qualified pipeline in the first 90 days by automating their prospecting and enrichment layer.

If scaling outbound is on your radar this quarter, would a 15-min walkthrough be useful?

### Being Genuinely Useful

**Subject:** found something

{{first_name}} – we scanned {{company_name}}'s current outbound footprint and noticed a few things that might be costing you reply rates.

Happy to send over the analysis. No pitch — just figured you'd want to see it.

Want me to share?

## Sequence Structure

When building a multi-email sequence, see `templates/sequence-structure.md`. Key rules:

- Email 2 threads to Email 1 (continues the conversation)
- Email 3 starts a NEW thread (new subject — feels like it came out of the blue)
- Email 4 threads to Email 3 (breakup/final touch)
- If Email 3 outperforms Email 1, swap their positions
- Vary the value prop across emails (time → money → risk)
- Each subsequent email should be shorter
- Breakup email goes last — signals you won't follow up again, often gets highest response rate
