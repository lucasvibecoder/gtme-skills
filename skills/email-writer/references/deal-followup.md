# Deal Follow-up Frameworks

Use this reference when writing emails to prospects you've already engaged with — active deals, post-call follow-ups, stalled conversations, ghosted prospects, or any situation where there's existing relationship context.

For first-touch emails to strangers, see `cold-outreach.md`.

---

## Core Principle

Deal follow-ups are about **momentum, not persuasion**. The prospect already engaged. Your job is to keep things moving, deliver on promises, and make the next step effortless.

Every follow-up should answer: **"What does this person need from me right now to move forward?"**

---

## Post-Call Follow-ups

The most common follow-up scenario. You just had a call. Now you need to send a recap, deliver promised resources, and confirm next steps.

### If Transcript or Notes Are Available

Scan the transcript for:
1. **Specific commitments made** — what did you promise to send/do?
2. **Pricing discussed** — exact numbers, not paraphrases
3. **Objections raised** — what concerns came up?
4. **Questions they asked** — especially ones you couldn't answer live
5. **Next steps agreed** — what's supposed to happen next and when?
6. **Other stakeholders mentioned** — anyone else who needs to be involved?
7. **Resources referenced** — case studies, docs, reports mentioned

### Post-Call Email Structure

```
{{first_name}} – [Short reference to the conversation — not "great call!" but something specific]

[1-2 sentences recapping the key takeaway or agreed direction]

[Promised resources or next step details]

[Clear next step with specific timing]
```

**Conditional logic:**

| What Happened on the Call | What to Include |
|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Pricing discussed | Include the exact numbers quoted. Consistency builds trust. Never round or paraphrase. |
| Demo or data test as next step | Include link, instructions, any prep they need to do |
| You promised resources | Attach them or insert `[ATTACH: {{resource}}]` placeholder |
| They need to bring in another stakeholder | "Would it be helpful to loop in [person] on this?" |
| No clear next step was set | Propose one with a specific date: "Would [day] at [time] work to [specific action]?" |
| Objection was raised but unresolved | Don't address it in the email — flag it internally for your next conversation |
| They asked a question you couldn't answer | Answer it now: "You asked about [X] — here's what I found..." |
| Calendar invite needed | Include link or propose 2-3 specific times |

### What NOT to Do in Post-Call Follow-ups

- Don't say "Great call!" or "Really enjoyed our conversation!" — it sounds like every other sales follow-up
- Don't recap everything that was discussed — they were there
- Don't introduce new topics or value props — stay focused on what was agreed
- Don't attach more than what was promised — don't overwhelm
- Don't CC people who weren't discussed — ask first

## Deal Stage Follow-ups

### Post-Discovery

They've told you about their problems. You understand their situation. Now keep momentum.

**Structure:**
```
{{first_name}} – [Reference something specific they said — shows you listened]

[1 sentence connecting their problem to how you can help — not a pitch, a bridge]

[Next step: "Based on what you shared about [specific thing], I think [next step] would be the most useful. Does [day/time] work?"]
```

**Key:** Reference a specific thing they said. Not "you mentioned some challenges" — but "you mentioned your team spends 3 hours prepping for each client call." Specificity signals you were paying attention.

### Post-Demo / Post-Proposal

They've seen the product or received a proposal. The decision is being made.

**Structure:**
```
{{first_name}} – [Acknowledge where things stand without being pushy]

[Address the most likely hesitation or open question — preempt it]

[Restate the specific outcome that matters to them — in their words, not yours]

[Next step — make it easy: "What questions came up on your end?" or "Is there anything you need from me to move forward?"]
```

**Key:** Don't ask "What did you think?" — it's too open and puts them on the spot. Instead, preempt the most likely concern: "I know [common hesitation] usually comes up at this stage — here's how we've handled that for [similar company]."

### Stalled Deal

Conversation was moving, then it stopped. Not ghosted yet — just slow.

**Approach:** Add new value. Don't just "check in" — give them a reason to re-engage.

**Tactics:**
- Share a relevant insight, article, or data point: "Saw this and thought of your [specific situation]..."
- Offer a new perspective on their problem: "Since we last spoke, I've been thinking about [their challenge]..."
- Reference something that changed in their world: "Noticed [company event] — does that change the timeline for [project]?"
- Ask a specific question that moves the conversation: "Last time we talked, [stakeholder] was evaluating [thing]. Where did that land?"

**Never:** "Just checking in," "Wanted to follow up," "Bumping this," "Haven't heard back"

### Ghosted Prospect

Multiple emails, no response. They're avoiding you.

**Option A: The Breakup**
Signal that you're done following up. This consistently gets the highest response rates (40%+ in many cases) because it removes pressure.

```
{{first_name}} – I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back, so I'll assume the timing isn't right.

If [problem you solve] becomes a priority down the road, I'm here.

Either way — no hard feelings.
```

**Option B: "Dropped the Ball?"**
Take the blame. Makes it easy for them to re-engage without feeling guilty.

```
{{first_name}} – I might have dropped the ball on this one. If I missed something or the timing shifted, totally understand.

If it's still relevant, happy to pick up where we left off. If not, no worries at all.
```

**Option C: Value-Add Re-engagement**
Don't ask for anything. Just give.

```
{{first_name}} – came across [specific resource/insight] and thought of [their situation].

[1 sentence on why it's relevant]

Figured you'd want to see it regardless of where things stand with us.
```

**Choosing between A/B/C:**
- Use A (breakup) when you've sent 3+ emails with no response
- Use B (dropped the ball) when the conversation was warm and then went silent
- Use C (value-add) when you want to keep the door open and have something genuinely useful to share

## Delivering Promised Resources

When the email's primary purpose is to send something you committed to:

```
{{first_name}} – as discussed, here's [resource name].

[1 sentence on what they'll find most useful in it — don't make them figure it out]

[If applicable: "Let me know if you have questions after reviewing. Happy to walk through [specific section] if useful."]
```

**Rules:**
- Lead with the resource. Don't bury it under pleasantries.
- Tell them where to look — "Page 3 has the ROI breakdown that's most relevant to your situation"
- Don't attach more than what was promised
- If you can't find or access the resource, be honest: "Still pulling together [resource] — will have it to you by [day]. In the meantime, [alternative or partial info]."

## Pricing Follow-ups

When pricing was discussed and you need to follow up with numbers or a formal quote:

```
{{first_name}} – here's the pricing breakdown we discussed.

[Include exact numbers — match what was said on the call precisely]

[If there are options/tiers, present them clearly but briefly]

[Next step: "Want to hop on a quick call to walk through this, or do you have what you need to evaluate?"]
```

**Critical:** If pricing was discussed on a call, the numbers in your follow-up MUST match exactly. If you said "$4K/month for the first tier" on the call, write "$4,000/month" in the email. Don't round, don't paraphrase, don't present a different structure. Inconsistency kills trust instantly.

## Multi-Threading

When multiple people at the company are involved:

- **Ask before CC'ing:** "Would it be helpful to loop in [person] for this part?"
- **When to create a separate thread:** If the new stakeholder has a different concern or role, start a fresh email to them — don't dump them into a long thread
- **When to CC:** Only for visibility, when the primary contact has indicated the other person should be included
- **Forward vs. CC:** If the primary contact says "Send this to my boss too," CC the boss on your next email. Don't forward the thread — it looks like you're going over their head.
