# Buyer Psychology

Frameworks for calibrating emails to how the prospect thinks, what they fear, and what moves them. These aren't templates — they're lenses. Apply them when deciding how to frame the email, not as fill-in-the-blank structures.

---

## Schwartz Awareness Levels

The most important calibration. If you get this wrong, nothing else matters.

### The 5 Stages

| Stage | Prospect's Mindset | What They Need From Your Email |
|-------|--------------------|---------------------------------|
| **1. Most Aware** | Knows your product, wants it, hasn't bought yet | Make it easy: price, offer, urgency. Remove the last barrier. |
| **2. Product-Aware** | Knows your product, not sure it's the best option | Show superiority: why you vs. the alternatives they're considering. |
| **3. Desire-Aware** | Wants the result but doesn't know your product exists | Name the desire. Connect it to your solution. "You want X — here's how." |
| **4. Problem-Aware** | Feels the pain but doesn't see a path to fixing it | Dramatize the problem. Make the status quo intolerable. Then hint at the solution. |
| **5. Unaware** | Doesn't know they have a problem | Don't mention your product. Call out their identity. Make them feel seen. Pull them into the conversation. |

### How to Identify the Stage

**Ask yourself:**
- Have they heard of us? → If no, they're Stage 3-5
- Have they heard of products like ours? → If no, they're Stage 4-5
- Do they know they have the problem we solve? → If no, they're Stage 5
- Are they comparing us to competitors? → Stage 2
- Have they requested pricing or a demo? → Stage 1

**In cold outreach, most prospects are Stage 3-4.** They want the result but don't know you exist, or they feel the pain but haven't connected it to a solution.

### Hook Strategy by Stage

**Stage 5 (Unaware):**
You cannot mention your product, your price, or even the problem directly. You must "call the market together" — identify who they are and make them feel seen.

Example: "To the VP Sales who just inherited a team of 15 and a quota that doubled."

This sells the email, not the product. The goal is to get them to read the next sentence.

**Stage 4 (Problem-Aware):**
Dramatize the pain. Make them feel the cost of doing nothing. Then hint at a better way.

Example: "Your SDRs spend 8 hours a week researching accounts manually. That's 400 hours a year per rep that could be pipeline."

**Stage 3 (Desire-Aware):**
Name the result they want. They know they want it — show them you can deliver it.

Example: "Generate 40% more qualified pipeline without adding headcount."

**Stage 2 (Product-Aware):**
They know about alternatives. Show why you're different.

Example: "Unlike [competitor], we [specific differentiator that matters to their situation]."

**Stage 1 (Most Aware):**
They want it. Make the offer irresistible.

Example: "We're offering [specific deal/trial/guarantee]. [Deadline if applicable]."

## Market Sophistication

How many emails like yours has this person received this month?

| Stage | What They've Seen | Your Strategy |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| **1. Fresh** | Nobody's pitched them this before | Be direct. Simple claim, clear offer. |
| **2. Competitive** | A few competitors have reached out | Make a bigger claim. Back it with proof. |
| **3. Saturated** | They've heard it all before | Pivot to HOW. Your mechanism — the specific way you deliver the result — becomes the hook. |
| **4. Exhausted** | Even mechanisms sound the same | Elaborate on the mechanism. Make it faster, surer, easier. Or find a completely new angle. |

**Most B2B inboxes in SaaS, sales tools, and marketing are Stage 3-4.** If you're selling outbound services, CRM tools, or anything in the "AI for GTM" space, assume exhaustion and lead with mechanism differentiation.

**For niche industries (industrial, healthcare, architecture, construction),** inboxes are often Stage 1-2. You can be more direct.

## Loss Aversion

People are 2x more motivated to avoid a loss than to achieve a gain. Use this.

| Gain Framing (Weaker) | Loss Framing (Stronger) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| "Save 5 hours per week" | "Your team is losing 5 hours per week to manual research" |
| "Increase close rate by 20%" | "How many deals are you losing because reps can't access the right data during calls?" |
| "We can help you scale faster" | "Without [solution], scaling will amplify every inefficiency you have today" |

**Rules:**
- Don't be alarmist or manipulative. Loss framing should be honest and specific.
- The loss should be something they can verify. "You're losing $X" is strong if X is real. "You might be at risk of..." is weak.
- Pair loss framing with a clear path to resolution. Don't just scare them — show the way out.

## Status Quo Bias

The biggest competitor is never another vendor — it's doing nothing. The prospect's default state is inertia.

**To break status quo bias, you need to make the discomfort of staying the same greater than the discomfort of change.**

**Tactics:**
- **Hidden costs:** Surface costs they don't realize they're paying. "Your team spends [X hours/dollars] on [task] — that's [Y] per year."
- **Compounding risk:** Show how the problem gets worse over time, not better. "Every month you operate without [solution], [metric] compounds."
- **Competitor movement:** "While you evaluate, [competitors] are already [action]."
- **Opportunity cost:** "The time your team spends on [low-value task] is time they're not spending on [high-value activity]."

## Cognitive Dissonance

When a cold email introduces an idea that clashes with a prospect's existing beliefs, it creates mental discomfort they're motivated to resolve — often by replying.

This is the psychology behind Poke the Bear questions (see `cold-outreach.md`).

**How it works:**
1. Prospect believes: "We have this handled"
2. Your question implies: "You might not"
3. The gap between belief and implication creates discomfort
4. They reply to resolve the discomfort — either to defend their position or to learn more

**Keys to making it work:**
- The question must be specific enough that they can't dismiss it
- It must be non-accusatory — you're curious, not attacking
- You must actually know something they might not — otherwise it's an empty provocation

## Applying Psychology in Practice

You don't consciously apply all of these to every email. They're lenses for making decisions:

- **"Should I lead with the problem or the solution?"** → Check awareness level. Problem-Aware = lead with problem. Desire-Aware = lead with solution.
- **"Should I be direct or subtle?"** → Check market sophistication. Fresh market = direct. Saturated = subtle, mechanism-led.
- **"Should I focus on what they gain or what they lose?"** → Default to loss framing unless the gain is genuinely extraordinary.
- **"How do I get them to respond?"** → Create cognitive dissonance with a specific, honest question they can't easily answer.
- **"How do I overcome 'we're fine as is'?"** → Surface hidden costs, compounding risks, or competitive pressure that makes the status quo uncomfortable.
